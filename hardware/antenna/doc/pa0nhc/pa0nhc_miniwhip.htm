        Best performance:
The original design of this Miniwhip was of pa0rdt, but showed IMD (mixing products at 72kHz, between 250 and 450 kHz, and on 7MHz).
 
I measured ca. 250mVpp output from two very strong high-powered MW AM transmitters at only 10km distance from me. This 100mVrms input already caused mixing products (f.i. 72kHz) and distortion of the modulation of the 625kHz transmitter..
Furthermore, in the evening, when strong short wave signals were received, a 5kHz raster of carriers showed up around the 7MHz amateur short wave band.
 
I redesigned the original to improve its IMD free large signal properties. Several test examples were made, with different antenna surfaces, and different electronics. 
 
A Jfet (like J31) has no protection on its gate. It cannot be driven with positive voltages.
Most Jfets have a low Idss and consequently a small input voltage range.
 
A dual gate MOSfet, with both gates interconnected as one gate, can easilly peak 40mA of Id,
It can  withstand more than - and + 8V input voltages without damage. 
A so connected fet is very linear (i think). 
Two external, fast diodes at the input, prevent damaging T1 if a very nearby TX is active, or lightning occurs.
 
The maximal input voltage with G1 and G2 in partallel is 8.6Vpp ( ! ! ), and max. output is 4.4Vpp ( "Gain" = -6dB).
 
All my versions did not show any spourï. The output was clean.
 
        How to choose the best type FET:
In the original "Miniwhip" a J310 Jfet was used for T1 with less current drawn in a higher value source resistor.
The impedance of the base of T2 is very lowZ. In order to be able to drive T2 properly T1 must be able to draw more than 30mA peak current @ 3V Vds into a 150 Ohms source resistor.
 
Years ago i already discovered that a dual gate MOSfet, with its gates connected together, acting as a MOSfet triode, is an excellent source follower, capable of up to 40mA peak source current, and high input voltage and undistorted output voltage. In the junkbox i found several 40673 dual gate MOSfets, they performed well, as BF961's did too. 
 
In the given PCB a BF961 dual gate MOSfet (SOT103 style) is soldered upside-down onto the bottom of the PCB. 
 
A TO92 style Jfet or dual gate MOSfet could be placed at the top with its legs though the hole.
 
A BF246A or B Jfet should work well. One 2N4859A Jfet could work too. Two BF245C Jfet in parallel could be useable. 
 
If the a Jfet of your choice has an Idss less than 40mA, reduce Vg1 (R1 higher value) so that Id equals half its Idss (measure it first).
This is not recommended as the max. input and output voltages will then be proportionally lower. But possibly still high enough.

Measure the Idss of a Jfet as follows:
- Connect g to s
- Connect a mA meter between -3V and s.
- Connect d to +3V.
- Measure the current. It has to be 40 to 50 mA.

The 2N3970, 2N4091, 2N4391, 2N4856A and 2N4859A have higher input capacitances, causing a little less signal strength. If one of these FETs is used, R3 must have a smaller value, f.i. 470 Ohms (Fc >= 50MHz). R3 is a stop resistor, preventing oscillation. It forms a lowpassfilter with the input capacitance of the fet. The -3dBpoint must be above 50MHz. 

        Biasing the FET:
You can use a Jfet or MOSfet of your choice, as long as it can draw 40mA @ Vds=3V & Vgs=0V (or Idss=40mA).

Set Is to ca. 20mA (Us=ca. 3.0 V) by choosing the appropriate value for R1. For a Jfet R1 must possebely have a HIGHER value (3M9 - 5M6).

      VLF/LW/MW freq. response:
R1/2 form a high pass filter with the parasitic antenna capacitance (ca. 10pF ?).
 
If R1 and R2 both are ca. 10k, frequencies below 3.5MHz will be attenuated (6dB/oct). This could help if a very strong MW or LW broadcast transmitter nearby is overloading your receiver input circuit. 
 
L1 shoud resonate to 29MHz with the antenna and the electronics input capacitance, in order to keep good sensitivity up to 30MHZ. Above 30MHz the freq. char. rolls off steep. It helps to attenuate very strong VHF/UHF signals which could cause problems.

 

    PCB and circuit design:
You could build the Miniwhip using the "dead bug" construction (see earlier prototypes). But the (double sided) PCB is designed so, that it also can be used single sided (bottom copper only), and with different styles of fets.
 
The PCB is best made double sided, with (where ever possible) all mass connections soldered at BOTH PCBsides. This improves stability. Certain components then must be lifted a bit at their mass connection, in order to be able to be be soldered at both copper surfaces.

The FET impedance converter is optimally biased for the best large signal handling. Again: the fet must have a Idss of 40mA @ Uds=3V. It is a source-follower, with a gain of -6dB. 
 
With a dualgate MOSfet and separate biased g1 and g2, the signal loss is less but the max output is far less. It is NOT the optimal solution. 
 
If you want more signal, then enlarge BOTH the antenna surface AND the mass surface of the electronics equally. Penalty: your receiver is easier overloaded. S/N will not be better.
 
T2 is a common VHF low power transistor: 2N3866. It also is optimally biased for processing the full signal levels delivered by T1. It is directly seating on top of the insulated top copper layer island, and sodered to it, thus cooled by the PCB. A cooling-star is then not needed.
R8 again is a stop resistor, it forms a low pass filter with the input capacitance of T2, lowers gain at rising frequencies, and discourages VHF-oscillation.
 
The signal splitter  (L3,4 etc) is placed directly near the receiver. It must be in a closed METAL box. 
It is wise to put ferrite clamps over all the cables there also.

TIP: This antenna records the difference in field strength between antenna an mass surfaces. The field strength difference is greater when the distance between both antenna and ground surfaces are larger. To enhance signal strengths, you could widen the distance between the top (antenna) and bottom (ground) copper surfaces. Or make the antenna surface (length) wider. To wide will restrict the high frequency response. But remember: not the absolute signal strength is important, the signal to noise ratio is. Stronger signals can lead to IMD in your receiver (not in this antenna !).

 
        Cabling:
For noise free reception, special care must be paid to the installation of the RG174 (RG58C) signal cable !
 
The impedance converter T1 can only discriminate the signal DIFFERENCE between the antenna surface and the mass surface of the electronics.
 
The mass surface of the PCB, and the outside of the screening of the signal cable act both as a counterpoise for the antenna surface on the PCB.
 
Any (man-made) noises induced ( or running) on the OUTSIDE of the cable screening, will try to run onto the mass surface of the Miniwhip.

Consequently, these noises will then be seen by T1 as a signal difference to the antenna surface. And WILL BE SEND BACK THROUGH THE INSIDE OF THE SIGNAL CABLE down to the receiver. 

Result: noise on the outside of the cable screening is heard in the receiver.

        IMPORTANT:
Prevent noises on the outer surface of the coax can run upwards from the receiver to the Miniwhip.

       Installation of the Miniwhip and de-noising :

IMPORTANT: This capacitive receiving antenna receives by detecting the DIFFERENCE in field strength between the antenna top part, and the "ground" bottom part. The "ground" part MUST be connected to a RF-CLEAN grounding. If noises are on the lower "ground" part of the Miniwhip, they WILL be heard in the receiver.

        The best solution :

- DO NOT use switched power supplies. They can be strong jamming transmitters.
- Install the antenna in a noise-free place, as far away from noise sources as possible,
- as high as possible,
- on a metal pole which is connected to its own noise free ground (NOT to mains safety ground),
- burry the coax in de ground (20 cm deep or more).

The purpose of much height is : Maximizing signal strength, and minimizing noise reception,
The purpose of good grounding is : Draining noisy mantle currents on the outside of the coax cable directly to ground.
The purpose of burying the coax is : Damping mantle currents.

      If you cannot burry the coax :
Run the coax along a grounded metal surface or over ground surface (if possible). The stray capacitance between cable and surrounding damps mantle currents.

Cable losses on short wave frequencies are minimal, because the coax is terminated correctly at the receiver end. The S/N will not be influenced. Don't worry about cable losses, except when listening on 28MHz in very silent circumstances where you have to use the receivers pre-amplifier.

Use thin,  3-5mm dia, good quality (double shielded) coax. Thin coax can be wound more times though the hole of ferrite cores, they  then are more effective.

Wind a few meters of the coax to a air coil (10-30cm dia), acting as a mantle current choke.
At several places, wind the coax several times through BIG ferrite clamps (with 13 or 16mm hole).
REM: the cable MUST lay loosely in the hole. Both halves of the ferrite core MUST be in good mechanical contact to each other !

In some experiments i found that, even if the signal inside the coax is clean, RF currents on the outside of the coax cable could be heard in the receiver. Strong noises on the OUTSIDE of cable screening (from switched power supplies or plasma TV) could penetrate the cabinet of my TS-570D. They could still be heard in the receivers weak background noise.
 
Test for penetrating noises by connecting a long piece of coax running through the house, terminated at the far end with a good dummy load. Listen for noises. Then put a coax ferrite clamp over the cable directly near the receiver. Hear what the difference is.

Also put (directly near the RX) a ferrite clamp over the 12V supply cable to the receiver.

Put a ferrite clamp over the mains cable of the 12V power supply to the receiver.

If the antenna is grounded well, disconnect the mains safety ground from the power supply and the receiver.
